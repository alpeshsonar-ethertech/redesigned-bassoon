ChokePoint — How it works

Theme: Poor Visibility on Parking-Induced Congestion — turning a parking-enforcement log into a next-shift patrol plan

← Back to dashboard

Bengaluru Traffic Police enforce parking reactively: officers patrol, ticket what they see, repeat. There is no map of which illegal parking actually chokes traffic, and no easy way to prioritise where to send limited patrols. ChokePoint solves exactly that — using only the enforcement log they already collect.

Violation log→ Clean & score impact→ Find hotspots + gaps→ Forecast tomorrow→ Patrol roster

1The core insight: enforcement data is biased

A ticket log tells you where officers went — not where the problem is. Rank zones by ticket count and you just rank where patrols already are. Send more patrols there, write more tickets, and the ranking "confirms" itself. This is a known trap in predictive policing called a feedback loop (Lum & Isaac, 2016).

What we do differently: instead of raw ticket counts, we measure impact per patrol-visit. A zone that's heavily ticketed by many officers may just be over-patrolled; a zone with high impact but only one or two patrolling devices is a real choke point being missed. That correction is the heart of ChokePoint.

2Quantifying congestion impact (not just counting)

Not every illegal park hurts traffic equally. A truck on a main road blocks a lane; a scooter in a quiet lane barely matters. We score each violation:

Impact = Vehicle footprint × Offence severity × Road-class
  • Vehicle footprint — heavy vehicle 5, car/medium 3, two-wheeler/auto 1 (how much road it blocks).
  • Offence severity — on a main road / double parking 4, no-parking 2, defective plate 0 (not a congestion issue).
  • Road-class — ×1.5 on main roads, ring roads, junctions, flyovers; parsed from the location text (no map API needed).

Summed over a zone, this is the Congestion Impact Score (CIS) — an estimate of how much a place chokes traffic.

Honest scope: the data has no live traffic feed, so CIS is an estimate derived from violation characteristics, not a measured speed reduction. We say "estimated impact" throughout.

3Cleaning the data first

Three corrections most people miss, all visible in the numbers:

  • Timezone: timestamps are UTC. Converted to IST, the real enforcement peak is 10–11 AM, not the 4 AM the raw data appears to show.
  • Sweep de-duplication: one officer ticketing a row of cars in one minute is a single enforcement event, not many independent signals — so we collapse exact re-logs.
  • Validation: rejected and duplicate tickets are excluded from impact (but kept for the quality analysis).

4Forecasting tomorrow — at the right grain

The key insight: predictable signal lives at a coarse spatial grain. Which exact corner is worst rotates day to day (noise), but the ~5 km area that carries the load barely moves. So the lever for accuracy is the spatial unit, not model complexity. ChokePoint forecasts on three nested grains, top-down by how reliable each is:

  • 📍 WHERE — map zones (~5 km): a rank-normalized blend per zone (all-history + last-28-day + last-7-day + same-weekday). The flagged 25 zones held ~95.8% of the next day's load, averaged over 38 days the model never saw (best any plan could do: 98.3%). The signal is so stable that a chronic-total-only ranking lands within a point of the full blend — evidence it isn't overfit.
  • 👮 WHO — police teams: each zone routes to the station(s) that cover it. A LightGBM LambdaRanker (grouped by date × 2-hour window, trained only on dates before the cut) orders the per-shift picks; the held-out top-10 station hit is ~6.8/10.
  • 🎯 EXACT — corners (~150 m): inside each flagged area, the few corners where the load piles up. Naming them captures ~47% of the area's load — the precise daily #1 rotates, but the set is stable.

5From prediction to action: the patrol plan

Each station has its own team. When a station's area is predicted quiet, its team is redirected to the nearest choke point that needs help — so coverage follows the hotspots with no extra manpower. The deployment view turns this into the exact corners to stand at, a call-in-help schedule by time window (which neighbour team to pull in, and when), and a shift-by-shift playbook — the artifact a sergeant actually carries.

Honest line: the "Coverage gaps" view reports coverage gained — measured from the data — when patrols are shifted from over-served areas to the busier ones they're currently missing. Actual congestion reduction is an estimate under the hot-spot-policing assumption that enforcement presence reduces violations at a location.

6Bonus the same data supports

Because the rejected tickets are kept (just excluded from impact), the same pipeline can flag areas with abnormally high rejection rates — a signal of unclear or low-quality enforcement worth re-siting. It's an internal-operations insight derivable from the identical log, with no extra data.

7The six screens

The police-station selector at the top scopes every screen: pick All Bengaluru for the city view, or one station to drill into it (one grain finer — its corners instead of zones).

  • 📡 Day replay — a real recorded day played back in 30-minute steps: worst spots as they build, the moments that mattered, an end-of-day summary.
  • 🗺 Obstruction map — every hotspot sized by estimated impact, with a toggle for spots police are under-covering.
  • 🎯 Coverage gaps (today) — where patrols go vs where the real load is, and the same-day moves to fix it (cross-station for the city, within-station for one station).
  • 📅 Tomorrow's forecast — the WHERE zones, each routed to its WHO team(s), then "Was the forecast right?" on the unseen next day.
  • ♟ Tomorrow's deployment — the plan: exact corners, a call-in-help-by-time card, the shift playbook, and "Did it work?".
  • 🧪 Predict from your data — upload your own enforcement CSV and get the same zones + teams + corners for the day after your latest record.
